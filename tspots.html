The entertainment capital of northern Luzon and is known as the "City of Angels." There is a saying that "It is the city where many dreams come true and also where many hearts are broken."

The population was 462,928 according to the 2020 Census. This accounted for 3.73 percent of the total population of Central Luzon. Mt. Arayat National Park

Considered as one of the most accessible parks from Manila, the mystical mountain of Mt. Arayat lies within the municipalities of Arayat and Magalang in Pampanga. At the summit is a circular volcanic crater, and at its foot is the Pampanga river, the second largest in Luzon, which traverses the provinces of Bulacan, Pampanga, and Nueva Ecija
Directions can be accessed by clicking here.

Bale Batu

In the lush forests of Mount Arayat, a majestic stone-structure complex stands. Dubbed the “Little Cambodia of Pampanga,” Bale Batu has been mesmerising its visitors for years now. “Bale Batu”, kapampangan for stone house, is a small stone-structure complex with a temple-like house similar to that of Cambodia’s Angkor Wat. It was built by the late Jesus “Apung Susing” Padilla who spent more than half of his entire life working on the project.

Culture & Tradition

Arayat has delicacies created and made by their fellow natives. One of their specialties is Baruya, which looks like a potato pancake but is made of papaya and small shrimp instead of potatoes. In Arayat, people are very sociable with one another. People usually leave their homes to spend their free time with their friends. Instead of watching TV and playing video games, most kids would play traditional Filipino games like sipa, piko, luksong baka in the streets near their homes. Some would play basketball in the public basketball court, while others would catch and breed roosters so their parents would have something to use for their sabong (cock fight) habits.

Culture & Tradition

This Good Friday procession in Bacolor is one of the most solemn, a perfect example of a traditional Filipino prusisyon with nothing but voices praying the holy rosary and solemn songs in between. While the Makatapak Festival (Barefoot Festival) is a street dancing competition in which competing groups colorfully dance barefoot along the processional route or "paglimbunan." It is commemorated at the town fiesta in November. "Makatapak" means "barefooted" in Kapampangan, and one of the highlights of Bacolor's town fiesta is the Miss Bacolor Pageant, which showcases the town's rich culture.

History of Guagua

According to records dating back to 1590, Wawa (Lán-nâng: Hua-hua), which means "river mouth" (Kapampangan: alua or bukana), was the earliest recorded form of the town's name. The town is strategically located along a river that was important for trade and transportation during the precolonial period. Wawa was already a prosperous settlement when Spanish colonists took control of it in 1561, renaming it Guagua, a Hispanised form of the original name. Archeological artifacts discovered in a nearby town confirmed the existence of a prehistoric community in Guagua.

Culture & Tradition

People in Mabalacat always celebrate Caragan Festival, which is held every third week of February as part of the month-long celebration of Mabalacat, Pampanga's town fiesta. This is named after a Mabalacat town chieftain who is an Aeta native married to a Mabalaquena named Laureana Tolentino. The celebration of traditional Aeta culture is the event's highlight. Pastorella, also known as Misa de Pastores, is also celebrated in honor of the shepherds at the birth of Jesus Christ. This is a collection of Latin hymns from the nine-day Christmas Masses.

Masantol, originally a part of the ancient Macabebe town, was founded as a separate town and renamed San Miguel on May 1, 1878, consisting of the former Macabebe barrios of Bebe, Bulakus, Kaingin, and Nigi. History of Minalin



Minalin's name was derived from the legend "minalis la ding dutung, minalis ya ing pisamban" (the lumber moved, and so must the church). Stocks of lumber at Sta. Floods carried Maria to a hilly Burol for church construction. Capitan Diego Tolentino scribbled "minalis," and thus the name Minalin was born.

History of San Luis

San Luis town was originally called Cabagsa or Cabagsac, a contraction of “cabag” (fruit bats) and “bagsac”, hence a place where fruit bats come to roost. It was later known as San Nicolas de Cabagsa to honor the memory of one of its cura, Fray Nicolas de Orduno. Dna is credited with the final name, San Luis. Luisa, the wife of a lawyer who represented the town of Santa Ana (then Pinpin) in claiming a parcel of land that was disputed by the neighboring town. San Luis Gonzaga (St. Aloysius Gonzaga, 1568-1591), a blue-blooded Jesuit saint who died at the age of 23, was the inspiration for the town's name. On April 25, the town holds its fiesta.

Sta Ana was previously known as 'Bale Pinpin,' which translates as 'laid aside. The name came about after the town lost several barrios to Arayat, Mexico, and San Luis in 1810. It was originally part of vast tracts of forest land, primarily bamboo groves, stretching from Apalit to Betis.

History of Sto Tomas

Santo Tomas was separated from its parent city of Minalin, Pampanga, on September 15, 1792. It was ceded to San Fernando, Pampanga on January 2, 1905, until 1905. On October 12, 1951, Elpidio Quirino issued Executive Order No. 476, which established Santo Tomas and its five barrios of San Matias, San Vicente, San Bartolome, Santo Rosario, and Santo Tomas, with the seat of government in barrio San Vicente.
